# 18 — Analogous real-world cases (HM has happened before)

Real cases — from news and the peer-reviewed literature — that mirror HM's: a **non-neurosurgeon forced to do an emergency burr hole / craniostomy for an epidural hematoma**, the **"talk-and-deteriorate" lucid interval**, **phone/improvised guidance**, and **remote/LMIC settings with no neurosurgeon**. These prove the scenario is real, recurrent, and survivable — and several map onto Kyro's exact value (and its exact gap). Use them in the pitch's problem/innovation slides.

> **Why this matters:** the panel will believe a *cited, real* case far more than a hypothetical. Two of these (Carson and Richardson, both 2009) are the rhetorical bookends: one shows phone-guided non-specialist evacuation *works*, the other shows what happens when EDH isn't recognized in time — even with money and access.

---

## ⭐ The near-perfect analog: a non-neurosurgeon, a household drill, phone guidance, an EDH
**Dr. Rob Carson — Maryborough, rural Victoria, Australia (2009).** A 13-year-old boy (Nicholas Rossi) fell off his bike, hit his head, **seemed fine, then began slipping in and out of consciousness — the classic EDH lucid interval.** The small rural hospital had **no neurosurgical drill**, so Carson — a GP/emergency doctor who had **never done the procedure** — grabbed a **household drill from the maintenance room**, **called a Melbourne neurosurgeon who talked him through it by phone** (where to aim, how deep), and **drained the clot**. The boy was airlifted out and **walked out of hospital on his 13th birthday.**
- **This is HM's case almost exactly** — non-neurosurgeon, improvised drill, phone-guided, EDH, lucid interval — *just in a high-income country with a working phone line.*
- **The Kyro point:** it worked **because the call held.** HM's didn't. Carson got lucky on connectivity; Kyro is what you use when you can't.
- Sources: [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna30834478) · [Irish Times](https://www.irishtimes.com/news/doctor-uses-domestic-drill-on-boy-s-skull-to-save-life-1.767156)

## "Talk and die" — what happens when the EDH *isn't* caught in time
- **Natasha Richardson — Mont Tremblant, Canada (2009).** Actress; minor-seeming skiing fall, initially declined care, **talked and felt fine (lucid interval), then deteriorated hours later and died of an epidural hematoma.** The canonical "talk and die" case — **proof that wealth and access don't save you from an unrecognized/delayed EDH.** (Widely documented; same year as the Carson case — the two together are the perfect contrast.)
- **"An Unfortunate Incident of 'Talk and Die': A Case Report"** — a peer-reviewed real case of the lucid-interval-then-collapse pattern. [PMC12432533](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12432533/)
- **Clinical backbone:** mortality for *unrecognized* EDH reaches ~40%; the lucid interval occurs in ~10–33% of EDH and can last 1 h to days. [StatPearls EDH](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK518982/) · ["talk-and-die" review](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23330993/)
- **The Kyro point:** recognition + acting in the window is everything — Richardson is the cost of Delay 1/3; HM survived only because *someone* acted.

## Non-neurosurgeons doing emergency burr holes in remote/austere settings (it's a documented practice)
- **⭐ "Burr holes in the bush" — rural/regional Queensland, Australia (2023).** A study explicitly on **clinician preparedness for non-neurosurgeons performing emergency intracranial-haematoma evacuation locally** when transfer is delayed — the formal version of HM's scenario. [Emerg Med Australasia](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/1742-6723.14134)
- **Intraosseous-needle craniostomy cases (the frugal-improvised cluster):**
  - **17-year-old after a car crash**, neurosurgery unavailable, transfer delayed by *weather*; an **emergency physician placed an intraosseous catheter intracranially** to decompress → **complete neurologic recovery.** (Weather-blocked transfer ≈ HM's blocked road.) [J Emerg Med](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0736467922003547)
  - **38-year-old** with an expanding EDH + herniation → **bedside IO-drill craniostomy + serial aspiration.** [World Neurosurg / PubMed 32634633](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32634633/)
  - **69-year-old, UK district general hospital**, EDH deteriorating before transfer → IO-needle craniostomy. [Injury](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0020138317300827)
- **Military doctrine:** the US Joint Trauma System CPG **"Emergency Life-Saving Cranial Procedures by Non-Neurosurgeons"** — the practice is formally protocolized for deployed/austere settings. [TCCC](https://tccc.org.ua/en/guide/emergency-life-saving-cranial-procedures-by-non-neurosurgeons-cpg)
- **The Kyro point:** non-specialists *already* do this and patients *recover* — Kyro doesn't invent the practice, it structures and de-risks it.

## LMIC non-specialist EDH evacuation (HM's exact setting, with outcomes)
- **⭐ Uganda (SW Uganda, 2025):** a **mid-level medical doctor** safely evacuated a **large vertex EDH via single-piece craniotomy** in a rural center when transfer was impossible; **GCS recovered to 15/15 at 24 h.** The closest published *LMIC* twin of HM. [PMC11937668](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11937668/)
- **Philippines:** a **general-surgery resident performed emergency craniotomy for EDH after consulting a neurosurgeon** — documented task-sharing (the "task-sharing > task-shifting" model). (task-sharing cohort)
- **Cambodia:** **general surgeons trained** in emergency cranial neurosurgery → **acute EDH 93% favorable outcome at 3 months.** [PMC9179964](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9179964/)
- **Tanzania (KCMC):** **non-specialist burr-hole** evacuation of EDH/SDH → **75.6% favorable, 18.6% mortality**; "burr hole may be the best alternative to craniotomy in resource-limited settings." [PMC10714222](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10714222/)
- **DR Congo:** 54 operated EDH/SDH cases in a tertiary unit. [PMC12535179](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12535179/) · **Ethiopia (Dessie):** 1-yr prospective EDH outcomes. [Front Surg](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/surgery/articles/10.3389/fsurg.2025.1381042/full)
- **The Kyro point:** the practice + good outcomes are documented across LMICs — Kyro turns the *lucky/trained exception* into a *repeatable, guided standard*.

## ⭐ The closest *geographic* analog: high-altitude mountainous, a general surgeon doing neurotrauma
- **Ladakh, India — "the highest plateau state": a case series of neurotrauma managed by a *general surgeon*.** A remote, high-altitude, mountainous region with **no neurosurgeon**, where a general surgeon does burr-hole clot evacuation — essentially the **Indian mirror of Gilgit-Baltistan.** [PMC8224252](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8224252/)
- **The Kyro point:** mountainous, isolated, no-neurosurgeon neurotrauma managed by generalists is *a known regional pattern* across the Himalaya/Karakoram — directly supports scalability beyond Pakistan (Nepal, Ladakh, GB).

## What these cases prove (the pitch synthesis)
1. **The scenario is real and recurrent**, not a contrived vignette — it has a name ("burr holes in the bush," "talk and die") and a literature.
2. **Non-specialists already do this and save lives** (Carson, Uganda, Cambodia, Tanzania, Ladakh, IO-needle cases) — so Kyro extends an accepted practice, it doesn't propose a reckless new one.
3. **Carson vs HM is the whole thesis in one comparison:** *same procedure, same improvisation, same phone guidance — the only difference is whether the lifeline held.* Carson's call held; HM's dropped. **Kyro is the lifeline that can't drop.**
4. **Richardson is the cost of inaction** — even with wealth and access, an unrecognized/delayed EDH kills. Recognition + acting in the window is the game.
5. **Ladakh proves geographic scalability** — mountainous, no-neurosurgeon neurotrauma by generalists is a pattern across the high-altitude LMIC belt.
